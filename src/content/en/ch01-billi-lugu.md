# Chapter 1: Bill's Story (p. 1)

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p001]
War fever ran high in the New England town to which we young officers from Plattsburg were assigned, and we were flattered when the first citizens took us into their homes, making us feel heroic. Here was love, applause, war; moments sublime with intervals hilarious. I was part of life at last, and in the midst of the excitement I discovered liquor. I forgot the strong warnings and prejudices of my people concerning drink. In time we sailed for overseas. I was very lonely and again turned to alcohol.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p002]
We landed in England. I visited Winchester Cathedral. Much moved, I wandered outside. My attention was caught by a doggerel on an old tombstone:

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p003]

> "Here lies a Hampshire Grenadier,
> who caught his death
> drinking cold small beer.
> A good soldier is ne'er forgot
> whether he died by musket
> or by pot."

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p004]
An ominous warning which I failed to heed. Twenty-two, and a veteran of foreign wars, I went home at last. I fancied myself a leader, for had not the men of my battery given me a special token of appreciation? I imagined my leadership talent would place me at the head of vast enterprises which I would manage with the utmost confidence.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p005]
I took a night law course and obtained an investigator's position with a surety company. The thirst for success had been kindled. I intended to prove to the world I was important. Business took me to Wall Street, and gradually I took an interest in the stock market. Many people lost money — some made fortunes. Why not I? I studied economics and business as well as law. Potential alcoholic that I was, I nearly failed my law course. At one of the final exams I was too drunk to think or write. Although I wasn't yet drinking steadily, it worried my wife. We had long talks during which I dispersed her misgivings, telling her that the best projects of geniuses had started out drunk; that the greatest philosophical reflections had such beginnings.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p006]
When the courses ended, it was clear law was not my field. The intoxicating whirl of Wall Street had me in its grip. My heroes were the leaders of the business and financial world. From such a mixture of alcohol and speculation I began to forge a weapon that one day would turn on me like a boomerang and nearly cut me to pieces. Living modestly, my wife and I saved $1,000. We put it into securities, then cheap and rather unpopular. I rightly calculated they would one day rise greatly in value. I failed to persuade my broker friends to send me out to inspect factories and enterprises, but my wife and I decided to go anyway. I had developed a theory that most people lost money on stocks because they didn't know the market. I later discovered many other reasons.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p007]
We resigned our positions and set off on a motorcycle, with a sidecar holding a tent, blankets, a change of clothes, and three huge volumes of a financial reference. Friends thought our heads needed examining. Maybe they were right. I had been lucky in speculations, so we had a little money, but in between we worked a month on a farm to avoid spending our small capital. For a long time that was my last honest physical labor. Within a year we had covered the entire eastern United States. By that year's end my reports to Wall Street had secured me a position and a fat bank account. Using options brought in even more, and by year's end I had cleared several thousand dollars.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p008]
Over the next few years, fortune showered me with money and applause. I had arrived. My judgments and ideas were followed by many, amid the rustle of bills. The great bull market of the late 1920s boiled and bubbled. Alcohol was becoming an important and exciting part of my life. There was loud talk in the downtown jazz bars. Everyone spent thousands and babbled about millions. Scoffers could scoff and be hanged. I had plenty of drinking companions.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p009]
My drinking assumed graver proportions, lasting all day and nearly every night. My friends' remonstrances faded one by one and I became a lone wolf. Many sad scenes took place in our fine apartment. Actual infidelity never happened, because fidelity to my wife, sometimes aided by extreme drunkenness, kept me from the worst.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p010]
<not-a-list/>In 1929 I caught golf fever. We went to the countryside, and my wife began to applaud me while I tried to outdo Walter Hagen. Alcohol caught up with me much faster than I caught Walter. I began to feel anxious in the mornings. Golf gave me a chance to drink every day and every evening. It was grand to saunter playfully over lush courses I had looked upon with such awe as a boy. I acquired the flawless tan only the wealthy wear. The local banker watched with amused skepticism as I flashed fat checks in and out of his till.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p011]
<not-a-list/>In October 1929 hell unexpectedly broke loose on the New York Stock Exchange. At the end of one such hellish day I stumbled from a hotel bar into a brokerage office. It was eight in the evening — five hours past the market's close. The ticker still clattered. I stared at the tape marked XYZ-32. That same morning the figure had been 52. I was finished, and so were many of my friends. The papers told of men jumping to their deaths from the tall towers of finance. I despised it. I certainly wasn't going to jump. I went back to the bar. By ten that morning my friends had lost millions — so what? Tomorrow is a new day. Drink brought back the old wild, determined will to win.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p012]
Next morning I phoned a friend in Montreal. He still had plenty of money and thought we should come to Canada. By the following spring we lived as before. I felt like Napoleon returning from Elba. No St. Helena for me! But drinking caught up with me again, and my generous friend had to let me go. This time we were broke.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p013]
We went to live with my wife's parents. I found a job but lost it through a brawl with a taxi driver. Fortunately no one could then have imagined that for the next five years I would find no proper work and scarcely see a sober moment. My wife took a job in a department store, coming home exhausted to find me drunk. I became a pest hanging around brokerage offices.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p014]
Alcohol was no longer a luxury; it had become a necessity. Moonshine, two bottles a day, often three, became the rule. Sometimes a small trade brought in a few hundred dollars and I could pay my bar and liquor-store bills. So it went endlessly, and in the mornings I would wake very early, trembling badly.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p015]
In the morning I needed several glasses of strong liquor before I could even get my hands more or less under control and dare to eat breakfast. Moreover, I concluded that years of drinking had impaired my thinking. Drinking which required effort on my part for the rest of the day too. For a businessman, such a state engendered many needless fears. Drinking captivated me without pause. The insidious insane notion that I might still take another drink without anything bad coming of it gripped me ever more often.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p016]
There came moments when the dark tide rolled back. When depression of sufficient speed and depth drives us to the bottom and new substances begin to be pushed in from somewhere, we likewise rise up and are in good spirits and whole. But then came the terrible moments of truth with the ice clinking in the glasses we held, thinking: "This time I won't drink myself silly. I'll quit at the right moment. I'll punish my liver just a little more..."

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p017]
I no longer made long or specific pledges of sobriety. My confidence was gone. I couldn't roll along with a conversation. Sometimes I had thoughts of suicide and wished I had the courage for it. The thought came whether I was insane. My unusual behavior during and after drinking confirmed it.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p018]
These dark feelings passed and I felt an irresistible urge to visit my first wartime friends. Old times came to mind. Suddenly I am in Paris, France, recovering from a multi-day drinking spree. My swaying is unbearable. I rent loneliness in a hotel room and sustain rolling daytime hangovers. Behind me lie those same old diary entries — the same old up-down-up-down as in an open well. My friend hinted quite modestly that perhaps some psychological or spiritual approach might bring a solution. How proud I was! How could I, with such a great store of knowledge, a good solver of family troubles, hear anything new from modern psychology?! No, from old days I had already acquired all sorts of theories.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p019]
But I listened to his talk, for I was glad of my old friend. Moreover, he expressed himself moderately; he was rather an example. He made it clear that I was mad again and that only humble belief in God could help. Thanks! Sometimes after morning coffee he himself took a glass or two. Of course he is out of date and cannot fully recover. Me? Ah, what — I pity myself again, well.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p020]
I became very thoughtful and depressed nonetheless. Perforce I had to admit defeat. How had it happened that everything had so hopelessly changed? I had been drunk for several days in a row. I understood in detail how quietly I had gone through madness a few days before, everything vanished, all was ended. I was already afraid of starting to see horrible visions. Fleeing back to clearer mind, I drank a trembling large gulp or two. Oh no, still in mood — I must try again. And then suddenly all belonging was gone from consciousness altogether.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p021]
Doctors kindly helped me to a hospital, where clarity came with difficulty and gradually. Dreadful were those first days. I knew it had once been very painful and that it could recur. And with horror I admitted that it worried me that I would soon get out of the hospital and drink again. I understood how mind and body deepen the calamity to ever greater proportions. My wife withdrew from the situation and remained quite unhappy. I of course did not want to start drinking, but at the same time I was sure I would.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p022]
My prejudice about alcoholism had now finally vanished. I understood clearly that the mind that believes one day it can control its drinking is the abnormal mind of an alcoholic. What mind is it that repeatedly places me in such painful extremity? One brandy, a cocktail, an ice-cold beer? Certainly not a normal mind. No one compelled me to take that first drink. What happens after the first drink is entered is quite another matter. But I could not somehow stop that first pair of drinks. As long as I have such a mindset, nothing awaits me in the future but hell, to which I am unwilling to go at any price. And then I understood.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p023]
When I reached such clarity, a sudden feeling came over me that someone was watching close by. It had a truly strange effect. Was it the moment when a shiver ran through me and my brows grew moist? Was a curtain drawn aside at that moment? Did I not see and feel a spiritual presence directly beside me? With the greatest sublime feeling, love, and forgiveness, an inspiration of clear perception seized me. I stood atop a mountain where a pure wind blew. God shows Himself to all who need Him, when they seek Him. He came to me and at last I saw, felt, believed. The veil of pride and prejudice had fallen from my eyes. A new world came into view.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p024]
The true meaning of what I had experienced at the Cathedral suddenly reached me. For a moment then I had needed and longed for God. I had a humble wish that He would be with me — and He came. But soon the sense of God's nearness had been extinguished in worldly clamor, especially that which went on inside me. And all had been left undone. How blind I had been.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p025]
In the hospital I was weaned from alcohol for the last time. Treatment seemed advisable, as I was showing signs of delirium tremens.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p026]
While in the hospital I humbly entrusted myself to the care of God as I then understood Him, that He should do with me as He willed. I gave myself unconditionally into His care and guidance. For the first time I admitted that alone I was nothing — that without Him I was lost. I looked my sins pitilessly in the face and wished my newfound Friend to take them from me, root and branch. Since that time I have not drunk.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p027]
A schoolmate visited me and I explained my problems and shortcomings to him thoroughly. We made a list of the people I had hurt or against whom I bore grudges. I expressed full willingness to go to these people and admit my wrong. I must certainly not criticize them. I had to clear up all such matters to the best of my ability.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p028]
I had to set my own thoughts beside a new inner awareness of God. Logical thinking thus became as if anti-logical thinking. In moments of doubt I had to sit quietly, asking only for guidance and strength to face difficulties as He wills. I should never pray only for myself, except when my wishes would allow me to be useful to others. Only then could I hope for reward. And I would then be rewarded abundantly.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p029]
My friend promised that when this was done, a new relation with my Creator would open to me; I would grasp the foundational truths of a life that offers a solution to all my problems. For this, faith in God's power was indispensable, along with sufficient willingness, honesty, and humility to establish and maintain a new order of things.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p030]
In words it all seems simple, but everything has its price. It meant the destruction of self-centeredness. I had to give everything into the care of the Father of Light who guides us.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p031]
These were pivotal and fundamental propositions, but the moment I fully agreed to them, their effect was like an electric shock. I was seized by a feeling of triumph, followed by such peace and serenity as I had never before known. There was a supreme certainty in it. I felt as if lifted up, as if the crisp wind of the mountaintop had blown through me. God comes to most people gradually, but to me His impact was sudden and profound.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p032]
Momentarily I became anxious and turned to my doctor friend to ask whether I was still in my right mind. He listened in amazement to my account.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p033]
At last, shaking his head, he said: "Something has happened to you that I don't understand. But you had better hold on to it. Anything is better than what you were." This good doctor now often sees men with similar experiences. He knows them to be genuine.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p034]
Lying in the hospital I thought there were thousands of hopeless alcoholics in the world who would perhaps be glad if they too could share what had so graciously been given me. Maybe I could help some of them. They in turn could help others.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p035]
My friend had stressed that the use of these principles must be expressed in all my doings. Especially important was working with others, as he had worked with me. Faith without works is dead, he added. And how terribly true that statement was of the alcoholic! For if an alcoholic cannot perfect and enlarge his spiritual life through work and self-giving, he will not survive the trials and tests ahead. If he does not work, he will certainly drink, and if he drinks, he will certainly die. Then faith would indeed be dead. With us it is just so.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p036]
My wife and I devoted ourselves with enthusiasm to the thought of helping alcoholics move toward the solution of their problems. I was lucky, for my former business colleagues remained skeptical for another year and a half, during which I found practically no work. My self-esteem was not at its best then and I was plagued by spells of self-pity and bitterness. Sometimes it would almost have driven me to drink, but I soon discovered that when nothing else helped, work with another alcoholic always saved the day. Many times in despair I have gone to my old hospital. But after a conversation with someone there, my spirits rise and I get back on my feet. It is a way of life that works in all difficulties.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p037]
We quickly formed friendships, and from this has grown a fellowship to which it is wonderful to belong. We know great joy in life despite all its tensions and hardships. I have seen hundreds of families set out on a road that truly leads somewhere; I have seen how even the most impossible home situations turn into normal ones; how every enmity and bitterness is swept away. I have seen men come out of asylums and take their place again in family and community. Businessmen and professionals recover their good name. There is rarely a suffering or hardship we cannot together overcome. In one western town and its surroundings there are thousands of members of our fellowship. We meet often, so newcomers can find the fellowship they seek. At such informal gatherings there may be 50 to 200 people at a time. We are growing in both numbers and strength.\*

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p038]
A drunken alkoholic is an unpleasant creature. Our trials with them are at times exhausting, at times comical, at times tragic. One poor man committed suicide in my home. He could not or would not understand our way of life.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p039]
Yet in all this there is also much that is merry. I think many would be shocked at our seeming worldliness and gaiety. But inwardly we are deadly serious. Faith must work within us and through us twenty-four hours a day, or else we perish.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p040]
Most of us feel that we need no longer hunt for Utopia. It is with us right here and now. My friend's simple talk across the kitchen table is multiplied day by day in an ever-expanding circle of earthly peace and goodwill toward men.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p041]
\*In 2001 AA had over 100,000 groups.

::para[ch01-billi-lugu-p042]
Bill W, one of the co-founders of AA, died on 24 January 1971.
