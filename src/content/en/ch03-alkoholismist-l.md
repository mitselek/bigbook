# Chapter 3: More About Alcoholism (p. 30)

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p001]
How, then, can we help our readers determine whether they belong among us? An attempt to give up alcohol for a time is certainly useful, but we believe we can offer the victims of alcoholism — and perhaps even medical professionals — something more. Accordingly we describe below some of the states of mind that precede a return to drinking, since plainly the heart of the problem lies just here.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p002]
What sort of thinking possesses the alcoholic who again and again repeats the desperate experiment of the first drink? Friends who have brought him to the brink of divorce or bankruptcy after a drinking spree, and who have tried to get him to see reason, are left at a loss when he heads straight for the bar. Why does he do it? Whatever is he thinking?

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p003]
Our first example is a friend whom we shall here call Jim. This man has a charming wife and family. He inherited a promising automobile business. He distinguished himself in the world war. He is a good salesman. Everybody likes him. He is intelligent, seemingly normal, except for a nervous disposition. Until his thirty-fifth year he did not drink at all. But within a few years he became so violent when drunk that he had to be locked up. On leaving the asylum he made contact with us.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p004]
We told him what we knew about alcohol and what solution we had found. He made a start. His family was reunited, and he went to work as a salesman for the firm he had lost through drinking. For a while all went well, but he could not cultivate the spiritual side of his life. To his consternation he found himself drunk six times in a row. Each time afterward we dealt with him and analyzed thoroughly what had happened. He agreed he was a true alcoholic and that his case was serious. He knew that if he kept on, the road to the asylum lay before him again. More than that, he would also lose his family, whom he deeply loved.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p005]
And yet he drank himself drunk once more. We asked him to explain how this had actually happened. His account ran: "I came to work Tuesday morning. I remember being irritated by the thought that I had to work as a sales agent for the company that was once my own. I had a little argument with my boss, but nothing serious. Then I decided to drive into the country to meet a possible car buyer. On the way I got hungry and stopped at a roadside eating place that also had a bar. I had no intention of drinking. I thought I'd have a sandwich. I also realized that in this place, familiar to me over the years, I might find a buyer for one of the cars. During the months I had been sober, I had often eaten there. I sat down at a table and ordered a sandwich and a glass of milk. Drinking still did not enter my mind. I ordered a second sandwich, and decided to take more milk as well.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p006]
"Suddenly the thought ran through my head that if I poured a dash of whiskey into the milk, it could do me no harm on a full stomach. I ordered whiskey and poured it into the milk. I dimly sensed that this was not a wise move, but calmed myself with the thought that I was drinking whiskey on a full stomach. The experiment went so well that I ordered another whiskey and poured it again into the milk. That too seemed to do nothing to me, so I took another."

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p007]
Thus began another of Jim's trips to the asylum. Here was the danger of commitment to treatment, of losing his family and his position, to say nothing of the enormous anguish and physical torment his drinking always caused him. He well knew himself to be an alcoholic. And yet all the reasons that forbade him to drink fell away easily before the foolish notion that he could drink whiskey so long as he mixed it with milk!

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p008]
However we put the precise term, we call this out-and-out insanity. How else could such a lack of proportion and of clear thinking be described?

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p009]
You may think this was an extreme case. To us it is not very different from the usual, for such thinking has been characteristic of every last one of us. Always there arose that peculiar twist of thought, in which alongside sane thinking there invariably appeared some insanely trivial pretext for taking the first drink. Sane reasoning failed to hold us in check. The insane thought won out. Next day we would ask ourselves, in all seriousness and sincerity, how it could have happened.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p010]
In some cases we have set out with the deliberate aim of getting drunk, feeling that nervousness, anger, worry, depression, jealousy, or something of the sort justified it. But even with such a start we have been forced to admit that, compared with what always happened, our pretexts for a drinking bout were insanely inadequate. We now understand that when we began drinking deliberately rather than by chance, we did not think particularly seriously or effectively about the possible dreadful consequences.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p011]
Our behavior with respect to the first drink is as absurd and unintelligible as a man's passion, say, for strolling on the roadway. He is thrilled by darting in front of fast-moving vehicles. Despite friendly warnings he enjoys it for several years. Up to this point he could be regarded simply as a fool with a strange idea of fun. But then his luck turns against him, and he is slightly injured several times in a row. One would suppose that if he were a normal person, he would give up such behavior. But soon a new accident befalls him — this time a skull fracture. Hardly a week has passed after his discharge from the hospital when a fast-moving streetcar breaks his arm. He assures you that he has resolved to give up his dangerous habit forever, yet within a couple of weeks he breaks both legs.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p012]
Accompanied by endless promises to be careful or to avoid strolling on the roadway, this behavior goes on for years. Finally he can no longer hold a job, his wife divorces him, and he becomes a general laughingstock. He tries every possible way to free himself of his fixation about strolling on the street. He has himself placed in an asylum in the hope of changing his habit there. But on the very day of his release he darts in front of a fire engine, which breaks his back. Such a man would surely be mad, would he not?

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p013]
You may of course think our example too ridiculous. But is it really? We, who have passed through that infernal machine, must admit that if strolling on the roadway is replaced by alcoholism, this example fits us exactly. However sensible we may have been in other things, we became strangely insane when alcohol came into the picture. These are harsh words — but are they not true?

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p014]
Some of you are thinking: "Yes, what you say is right, but it does not quite fit us. We admit we have some of these symptoms, but we have not gone to such extremes as you fellows have, and probably will not, because from what you have told us we understand ourselves so well that nothing of the kind can ever happen again. We have not lost everything in life because of drinking, nor do we intend to. Thanks for the information."

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p015]
This may hold for some non-alcoholics, because although they drink thoughtlessly and heavily at present, they are able to stop or limit drinking, since, unlike us, their brains and bodies are not yet damaged. But the actual or potential alcoholic, with rare exceptions, cannot stop drinking on the basis of self-knowledge. This fact we keep stressing in the hope that it may at last reach our alcoholic readers, as bitter experience has shown it must. But let us consider yet one more example.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p016]
Fred is a partner in a well-known accounting firm. His income is good, he has a pleasant home, is happily married, and is the father of capable college-age children. He has so amiable a disposition that he makes friends with everyone. You would look far for another businessman as successful as Fred. Outwardly a firm-minded, well-balanced man. Yet he too is an alcoholic. We first met Fred about a year ago at a hospital where he had gone to recover from a nervous breakdown. Such a thing had happened to him for the first time and he was deeply ashamed of it. Still less did he regard himself as an alcoholic — he assured himself that he had come to the hospital to steady his nerves. The doctor told him plainly that his case might be more serious than he himself thought. For a couple of days he was depressed about his condition. He determined to give up drinking once and for all. It never occurred to him that, despite his strong character and position, this might not succeed. Fred did not wish to believe he was an alcoholic, still less was he willing to accept spiritual medicine for his problem. We told him what we know about alcoholism. It interested him, and he admitted that some of these symptoms were indeed his — but he was far from willing to admit that he could not help himself. He was absolutely sure that this humbling experience, combined with the knowledge obtained from us, would keep him sober for the rest of his life. Self-knowledge would put things right.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p017]
For a while nothing was heard from Fred. But one day we were told he was back in the hospital. This time he was rather shaky. He soon let us know that he was eagerly awaiting our visit. The story he told us is extremely instructive, since he was a man who was fully convinced that he had to give up drinking, who had no pretext for drinking, and who in all other matters showed excellent judgment and firmness of purpose — and yet in spite of it he now lay flat on a hospital bed.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p018]
But let him tell his story himself: "Your words about alcoholism made a great impression on me, and I truly did not believe I could start drinking again. I was quite taken with your thought that the first drink is preceded by an insidious insanity, but I was sure that after what I had heard from you this could never happen to me again. I thought I was not so far gone as most of you, that I had always dealt successfully with my personal problems, and that now too I would succeed where you had failed. I felt I had every reason for confidence and that it was only a matter of using willpower and keeping vigilant.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p019]
In this state of mind I set about my business affairs, and for a while everything went well. I had no trouble at all declining the drinks offered me, and I was beginning to suspect that you were some kind of incurable neurotics who dramatize. One day I went to Washington on business. I had been sober at home for several months and was feeling physically wonderful. There was no drinking at the hotel and my timetable at lunch was rather tight. After lunch I had nothing more to do and thought of going back to New York. I walked through the station building. Grumpy thoughts came to me that I was now bored, I had to wait for the train. One way or another I would head toward the café. I thought I'd have one cocktail there and a bite of lunch. I only realized this thought on the stopover, and saw that the train was still an hour away. I calmly enjoyed a drink — and ordered another. After lunch I decided that instead of New York I would rather take an afternoon nap, just like that. When I awoke in the hotel room, evening had come. I walked through Washington and decided to stay the night after all.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p020]
Strolling along, stopping at a café, I ordered a couple of cocktails, then more, and went home only on the morning flight. I remember the new newspaper offers I was looking at started to have a real effect on me, and people were already calling me. Then I have a three-day blank in memory."

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p021]
Visiting him in the hospital, we asked how it happened that, after understanding all those principles, he had succumbed to the urge to drink. "Did you think during the night about what would happen if you drank? Did you have no line of defense?" He answered: "It didn't cross my mind for a moment what had happened before, or the warnings, or what you had said — none of that did I remember. The whole time I thought I could drink, and it was a rather ridiculous idea, because at that moment I wasn't considering the consequences at all. I forgot what I did in my own defense — to apply it to my spiritual illness of alcohol. I didn't think for an instant that such a habit, repeated ten to a hundred thousand times, would never again serve me well. My old pattern of thinking about the Washington trip did not come into my mind. I only knew that I could not keep myself sober by my own effort."

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p022]
Fred stayed in the hospital three weeks and left there dedicated to a way of life that saved him from years of alcoholic capitalism. And, as could be expected, some simple remedies proved effective. What we have noted as a link so far seems to us also to mean that we come to know someone higher than ourselves. Our next chapter lays bare its faults. We hope we have been accurate in how we further approach alcoholism's special capacity. This was a fairly accurate description of those who tried to weigh it.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p023]
If you ask why you cannot, like Fred, cross over to the other side, there are obvious reasons. You may well be one of those, and in some rare case, who can even stop when he wishes, so that in one way or another, all at once or step by step, he pulls through sober enough. You cannot control your drinking — or if you can, it was as easily given up as with Fred, if he too had had such a possibility. Of strange make are these circumstances.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p024]
It would be strange indeed that after a couple of drinks we feel we can somehow bring ourselves to heel, while we feel at the same time, deep in hopelessness, contrary to Fred, that we cannot even drink a little. That is how it is in the ruined man's head, which plainly declares from outside: "Taking the first drink is, of course, the stupidest thing I could do; but if I don't strain myself or get in a rage at two drinks that don't budge, then nothing is left. I know what a compelling thought-faculty it is."

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p025]
The last phrase secures the matter too firmly. We can very firmly state that although someone may yet be able to control, he has lost the ability to control. Every day of life we see drinkers who live at the moment when they decide to give up drinking forever. Without any real trial this can never be known.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p026]
In our view only one complete test is closed to us. If you meet, unawares, more than a thousand people whose story more threatens delay than you know, then know that we think you may be one at peace. Always be cautious about drinking. Many of them have exciting stories in reserve about how the will came back just at the time they were already drinking with a great rush of alcohol. They are not worth the thing. In the end they lose their minds to alcohol. With some we have been in a similar situation who stayed controlled for a month's time. We had to train them carefully and thus learn what works. In all those cases sober cessation ended yet again in plain drunkenness. Our surroundings and position have shown, understandably, how dangerous it is to treat alcohol as a ghost from which one can flee. We try to show you and clearly to have you sense that the danger is hard-to-grasp.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p027]
"Is there no time and place for drinking?" you may ask. "Yes, people drink successfully and do so very much. I can all the time hold off a few weeks to see the devastation on my consumption and test whether a problem arises, thus trying again to be a moderate and controlled drinker. I know I'll do so, because I know several people who overindulge just the same, yet pull around when it's needed."

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p028]
Doubtless about such people all may be true. A non-alcoholic cannot become an alcoholic. They are people who can stop or alter drinking when there is reason enough to do so. Yet we are still convinced that an alcoholic cannot drink under control, and that an alcoholic, drunk or with a drink, has no interest who stirs the whole drinking bout to dust.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p029]
You may think we have gone completely out of our senses. Perhaps you think we have come fully to understand ourselves, and somehow learn and can decisively drink with full awareness. No doubt one may go several weeks, or considerably more weeks later, without drinking. But the time will come when he drinks again. That is your test, whether you wish to try or not.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p030]
Knowing that thousands of alcoholics suffer on, is it worth the experiment? It may cost a human life to find out!

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p031]
In a true emergency there is no choice. We are, in humane terms, to risk the unavoidable drinking at alcohol's hand, one answer to the problems, or forgetting someone. We came to the realization that no one feels and no one can take a single drop to drink. We must remain in the clear world. Let us remember, we are speaking of those who cannot drink normally. There are two types of people — one to be treated — that is the alcoholic.

::para[ch03-alkoholismist-l-p032]
What then is the solution? We are at all events to rebuild a genuine life with willing hearts. And this is fully possible. But since we are alcoholics, we cannot skirt around this problem. Yet we have been able to work out such spiritual tools as help us to stand against the problem of alcoholism. We have come to know what it is, and we can cope with it. In the following chapter we describe, step by step, our approach.
