::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-title]

# Chapter 2: There is a Solution (p. 17)

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p001]
We, the members of Alcoholics Anonymous, know thousands of men and women who were once as hopeless as Bill. Nearly all have recovered. They have solved their drinking problem.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p002]
We are average Americans. Among us are people from every corner of our country and many walks of life, as well as people of many political, economic, social, and religious backgrounds. We are people who would ordinarily not mix. Yet there reigns among us such a sense of brotherhood, friendliness, and mutual understanding as is indescribably fine. We are like passengers on a great ship the moment after rescue from shipwreck — from stern to captain's table the vessel is filled with camaraderie, gaiety, and democracy. But unlike those shipboard travelers, our joy at escaping disaster does not fade as soon as each goes his own way. The feeling that we have been in the same danger together is one component of the strong bond that unites us. But that alone would never have joined us as we now are.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p003]
For each of us, the crucial fact is that we have found a common solution. We have a way out upon which we can absolutely agree, and upon which we may join in brotherly and harmonious action. Such good news does our book bring to those who suffer from alcoholism.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p004]
Such an illness — and we are convinced that it is an illness — takes hold of our fellow beings as no other malady does. If a person has cancer, all feel sympathy; no one is angry or offended. But it is otherwise with alcoholism, for it is attended by the utter destruction of all of life's values. It swallows up the lives that come into contact with the sufferer's. It brings misunderstanding, fiery resentment, financial insecurity, the disgust of friends and employers, the warped lives of innocent children, the sorrow of wives and parents — anyone can continue the list.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p005]
We hope this book will inform and bring comfort to all whom it touches or may touch. They are many.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p006]
Highly capable psychiatrists who have worked with us have found that it is sometimes utterly impossible to persuade an alcoholic to discuss his condition frankly. Strange too is the fact that we are often even more inaccessible to wives, parents, and close friends than to psychiatrists or doctors.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p007]
But a former problem drinker who has found the solution, and who is properly armed with knowledge of himself, can as a rule win the complete confidence of another alcoholic in a few hours. Until such an understanding is reached, almost nothing can be accomplished.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p008]
The person who begins to approach an alcoholic has experienced the same difficulties and clearly knows what he is talking about. His whole demeanor announces that he is a man with the right answer, that he has no saintly manner or other traits that repel alcoholics. When we discover a new candidate who qualifies among those we may help, we bring our whole store of knowledge to bear on presenting the problem. Usually he has found that everyone he knows loses patience with him and can never offer him help. But now there comes an advocate who has the same illness and who, on the contrary, brings with him a solution. As a group we have been spared the thankless labor that in other cases must fail from the start. Such people are moved to tears by the facts of their own lives, told in a way that forbids doubt of their sincerity. The actual difficulty facing them is usually healthily dispelled within a few hours. These new candidates feel that they have met and now know dozens of people whose thinking and experience are exactly like their own. It is the broad experience of each one, in which he tells his own story with an honesty and sincerity that excludes all criticism. As these stories unroll, all doubt falls away that the other person is able to offer help with his experience and knowledge. Recollections and pretense are dangerous, and they are not needed. Each opens up more and more.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p009]
Those who have trained themselves into such an unturnable resistance to the dreaded word _'spiritual'_ that it can no longer usefully be applied to our case — they need not still be bravely fended off from our claims of spiritual recovery. Many of us were just as critical. In one way or another, having laid our hands on a hard truth, we have felt at home in the sensing of spiritual power and direction and reality. To our minds, the former alcoholic's way of thinking is the least likely to mix these things up; neither are we in any way in the grip of illusion.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p010]
All of us keep our minds a little open: the most closed mind is most likely to bear up the worst.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p011]
Every alcoholic should read the pages that follow. We hope they will take an impartial view. We understand that simply because of his lack of sobriety it is harder for him to keep an open mind. If he becomes irritated reading that alcoholism is overcome only on the spiritual plane, we ask him to turn to the twelfth page of the chapter, where such a thought arises.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p012]
If you are an alcoholic who wants to recover, you may say: "What is the matter — I agree with everything. Of course I am open to everything. You have my consent to undertake this program, although right now there are some doubts whether it is fair. I would be ready to do anything, something greater, if it filled me as it has filled you."

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p013]
Every alcoholic wants his truth to be proved right, that he may hear it fairly. He waits for the first thing to happen. That is a fine attitude, and we understand that at this level we should be able to be the same.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p014]
We emphasize that we do not claim everything we recommend is right. But that implies some generalization. Some people, men and women, have felt not the slightest desire to submit to these methods. We do not try to deceive anyone. But over the years we have lived, and in recent years, we have learned that two classes of people are unable to apply the full substance of our program. They are people destined to be incapable of perceiving the attitudes that are essential to our recovery.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p015]
There are also people who suffer from severe emotional and mental disturbances, but if they are not otherwise disoriented, they achieve results.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p016]
Our life stories before and after make three important things clear:

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p017]

1. That we were alcoholics and could not manage ourselves.
2. That probably no human power could have cured our alcoholism.
3. That God could do it and wished to do it.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p018]
Having established an understanding of ourselves, we went on with the steps set forth on the following pages. Anyone may begin a relationship with the Creator of the Universe. We hope that from the outset you will have such insight that for others God has been characterized by some definite word or definition. This depends only on your attitude, your willingness to believe in a greater power, a power higher than yourself. As soon as we are fully ready, our faith answers to the steps we take.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p019]
We have had, little by little, some religious inclination, but we have also been thankful for it. If we had not wished to accept or learn new religious truths, we would never have grown weary of faith. It is not our role to define particular religious truths. We all believed that we had the genuine human right to form our own conception of God as we ourselves perceive it.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p020]
Whether we believe in it or not does not concern us particularly now, but we can ask ourselves: do we believe, or are we willing to believe, that there is a power greater than ourselves? As soon as a person has said that he believes or is willing to believe, we sincerely affirm that he has set out on the road. We have seen, again and again, that on this simple basic truth the person ready to rise has been at once as on the wings of the spirit.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p021]
After years of feverish, self-justifying atheism, we have found that it is only a small obstacle. The difficulties fall away only when this problem is no longer with us. If we refuse faith, this is no stopping-point for us. On our journey it is not a significantly great problem whether you believe or not.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p022]
But if you ask the question of whether you are willing to believe in this, then the steps are before you. Experience has shown that no one comes up short in finding that there is a greater power than himself, once he has truly examined the question. And this is especially so when the relationship with God is helpful right from the very beginning.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p023]
Agnostics and atheists are extremely self-sufficient people. We find that with anyone who has had tremendous good fortune, it is not easy for a human-divine estimation to arise at all. If you can in any way be an accepting recognizer of faith, then the work is drawn to its start. It happens that here the wheat is separated from the chaff. The question is simply: do you believe, or do you wish to believe? From our experience we can say that finding the right profession of faith is easy.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p024]
When we speak of this to you, the alcoholic, it may seem that your anger is rightly appropriate. It may be that you are angered when I tell you, the alcoholic, that the antidote to the illness is the spiritual one alone. But so it is — and especially so if you already had some old conviction or inclination, for then all the more is a greater intellectual struggle summoned up against it. We know that we had the same feeling as you. We who have intellectually considered spiritual problems found that God still seemed distant in our upper study. Therefore here are a few flashes of thought by way of encouragement. Besides the skittishness we should meet when we speak of God's powers, there are other enemies not dependent on you. At one moment you marvel that the spirit and the definition of God become simpler than any other intellectual derivation. And we ourselves — if anyone understands it more, we are still shy when the bank of materials is available for recognition and their composition is truer than anything else that is true.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p025]
When we explain how we see these things, please give us a little time. This would suit even one who already has firm religious positions. Even they would say that religion is enough, but they must apply it. In our approach, spiritual principles would draw us along with them if we truly believe they help in being useful to some of our offices' material values, while in no way have we found anything but the word — all the spiritual being set aside. If we can be exact and crystal-clear, to truly take up our faith, we think everything becomes much simpler.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p026]
In this book we present our personal experience and views. We can only offer you advice and suggestions. Certainly you will see that we do not wish to make religious propaganda. None of us wishes to do that. We think that each person of religious convictions has the right to draw his own conclusions. Each of us should form his own conception of this higher power which helps him.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p027]
If you resist accepting even these simple basic notions, perhaps you are a little troubled. Do not let that disturb you. Experience shows that this calmer mind can be shaped for oneself in time. Try to keep in mind the things you consider important, remain simply open-minded, and consider us. We ask only that you try to observe the results of applying these principles in our lives.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p028]
If you consider yourself an atheist or agnostic, or if you have withdrawn from every religion, then reading this book may seem a greater demand than you can bear in your self-possession. Probably you are right. We believed no human being could understand or believe without enough arguments on either side. What we consider especially likely is that you have set to reading this chapter caught between the fear of death and a sense of duty. You may be a tired person whose pride has suffered. Deep, deep within you — perhaps for the first time in life — a conviction has arisen that nothing else has worked. Something has been broken. We know that feeling too. We were all in debt to someone for something. Perhaps we know this ourselves. Do not give in to rage. We are glad that we have been brought low. When it happened, it was overpowering for us. We are not encouraged that they had more of a story than anyone thoughtful could have given us. We watched for when a story would come, and when it did, we were incomplete.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p029]
If contentment is important to us, we must first accept moral defeat. If we supposed that by sheer willpower and determination alone all could be set right, we see that this is a deceptive achievement. We work with human powers nevertheless, and so we find that at some point this becomes a natural attitude. We see that spiritual people are on the right path. We understand that many people, being on these paths, have achieved something and continue to achieve.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p030]
In fact we find nothing strange in our approach against which a reasonable person would object. Besides, why not convince oneself with the argument that those who possess abundant power ask, in critical moments, for spiritual strength greater than their own? Today we see that in the long run all this becomes entirely true. It is understandable in isolation that a restless, self-destroying, destructive selfishness will end as the sole failure.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p031]
Our wellbeing is founded on the literal translation of the English word 'ruin' — laying waste. Some critical moments should foresee that if the outcome is destructive, the situation is already catastrophic and doomed. Our whole way of thinking, positive or negative, has arisen from our own perspective. The time has come at some point to step between intellectual independence and surrender. This might sound to us like a fearful opposite, yet it means, on the contrary, a good balance.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p032]
A Steady Job
Do you ask why we believe that our story of God is trustworthy?
In our view there are many reasons. Some of them are as follows.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p033]
From practical experience we find that nothing has given as much joy as this spiritual compulsion to be at our best after all, and to act in the search. For many thousands of years people have felt that everywhere they had an inescapable rising, once they had acknowledged their inadequacy. Adjusting themselves in this way, they are united with a natural sensing outside our selves that, for the search for such power, we are whole. Building on a great mass of facts we tell you that we have never doubted this. If in former days pride attended us, then today your knowledge and skill are not great enough to obtain sufficient certainty. No at-all reasonable being regards himself as the whole universe, nor as the sole actor in its pattern. It is quite clear to him that there is a power outside his personal ken. Around him in the interstellar universe he sees separations so immensely certain, that move and hold at once. That these are in order — there lies a marvelous understanding. What we see in this is a mighty union of powers.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p034]
Our scientists show us that all these evident things display a theory so complex that no mind can read them fully. Its elementary observant system declares that powers rising in growing worlds, an unleashed life, a purposeful aim, and order in all things — it is now a sign that this balance can come about by intellectual will. Though there is room to doubt concerning human capacities, our mind and thoughts are undoubtedly a weighty signification. By every sign it cannot be otherwise.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p035]
If our life has received power from a world greater than ourselves, then this power must work in the openness of our life and in our intentions. If a person is thus in his right mind, then in countless ways God and Nature are being fulfilled and experienced in him always. Our faith is equally justified by millions of reasons. Even if we were alone and could experience life only on the visible evidence, we would still summarize that the coming of the Almighty proceeds from us outward and say that our small capacity must be inspired by Him… If this is our true conviction and not merely lip service, this is our first step in beginning. We find God is interested in us more than we think.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p036]
But then, after that… we remain in the dust. It is possible that earlier we had already met some warning from Him in our lives, heard it from our friends… Or — what a joke! — we thought, what of it? We too somehow believed, but did not feel the true warmth and closeness of the higher power that everyone talks of. Our answer is that if you cannot get enthusiasm in that way, it is still fine that you have come this far. Real faith teaches the belief in things which are true even though you do not see them. You can trust God, even where your mind tells you He is there.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p037]
Wherever and however you decide to find God, it must be so that you trust His judgment in your affairs. Having become aware of your own power, you must become aware of His power. At last you grasp your smallness and greatness, peace and strength, in a new and wondrous happiness. This is our experience. Anyone who tries it can experience it.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p038]
Often the person who already has religious convictions has the problem of being too overconfident. He thinks he has recovered only within the frame of faith and creed. He may indeed be religious, yet still emotionally out of balance. He may be so humanly proud of his high religiousness that he loses the aim elsewhere. For him faith has been merely an attempt to cling convulsively to something, and not the art of maintaining a clear mind, naturalness, and fairness in daily life.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p039]
So we have found we must place God in the first place if we wish to get on in life. Though some of our tenets of gospel seemed like exaggerations, we found the truth in the phrase: "Seek first the kingdom of God, and all these things will be given you as well." This is taken here literally.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p040]
When we put the great power first, the results were just in the manner of the medium that was led into us, which guided and held us in all things. Former alcoholics on seemingly very different planes often speak in one and the same language. Hear the grasp of what they told us — a single word is — We were reforged. We found what we had to do.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p041]
Let us not be so taken up with our tasks in the world; let a way of working find us. To do what we want to do and take what we can. A little such self-centered life, together with the best life on our own terms. Granted that in fact our program presumes the opposite. We understand we must give up our lives by including others, so that the consequences may be good. Often we have had to place the powers of others, or their needs and wishes, ahead of our own. We did this because we understand that if we do not, we cannot be saved. At such a decisive moment we are forced to set self-love as secondary, always aware that the soul-care that fills our own mind is on our plane.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p042]
It is justified demand, we are sure. We set our problem at a higher order than we could, and did not remain only in the snare of power. Our persistence told us that we had made ourselves dependent on a power — who was also God, who helps us and others. God was setting us free of doubt. As we confessed to Him, we had to receive further judgment still, for we had thoughts and deeds that strengthened us to the bottom of our hearts. On this we stood with Him.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p043]
Today we believe that He has unceasingly been calling us by nature to that which we, strenuously struggling, had long been restless to endure. Reasonable people are cheerful, like others. They do not contend that their continual struggles should be injured or made to suffer, that the world should stand before them on a fine afternoon waiting. They see others as partners who likewise have the right to live in the same reality. They know that those are necessary who together accomplish more than alone. In life this holds generally — all that is not precisely to be sought by signs. Nor should it be, for inwardly you know you help yourself more than anyone else could help you. This means, with God one remains in the best communion.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p044]
Today's world seems to us a dualism, mesmerized, bound by the words "I," "my," and "me." The origin of war is the wish to own how others behave, when they are treated the same. Failure in family, at work, in friendships — in every conflict the same elements show themselves. Even the fairer sex has, alas, never in everything been delivered from its own quarrels. We have all lived in such a world. Even at the societal level it is highly conflicted, and it reflects the hard inner life of every person. And people who have had to explain their mind to themselves, despite everything — who may even be drunk, in a fit of hysteria — should let go. On this thought we tear ourselves out of such a self-centered refuge where we nest precisely because we cannot see it through to the end. I may think I am clean, yet repeated evidence shows this is not so. I accept others' verdict that I too consciously try to give up my power for the benefit of others — thereby the solution draws near to me.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p045]
Our first steps toward other people belong to the way of kindness from the very beginning. Even when we are ourselves reluctant, we cannot count on going far unless we examine both sides of the problem. In people-matters it is the right act — not the prejudice — that is the useful process. Everything cannot be done rightly at once. Only then do we learn to walk, yet we stumble. But if we have truly good will, there is the will to see such great things grow quickly. Little by little we can make a stranger's friendly conversation a premise. With reasonable good will we can set aside our thousand resentments, hatreds, and jealousies. We can learn to be gentler and more patient when we understand someone about something. We learn to be more restrained toward those who would otherwise be driven mad. We can even endure — even when they are unjust.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p046]
The same tolerance toward the patient people we meet takes on a new form in life. At best we may consider that people may understand us better too, should it so turn out. Such tolerance is as life-affirming and necessary with formerly very emotionally troubled people as anyone sparsely numbered can be. We have spoken of what we should do. Let us see what we would not do ourselves.

::para[ch02-lahendus-on-ole-p047]
If you are as bottomless an alcoholic as we, then it is certain that nothing half-hearted we offer you can help any further. We were in a state where life had become intolerable and we had reached the very peak beyond which no human power is able to help. We have two choices: either to walk the road of madness to ruin, finding flight from pain even by pretending a little longer, or, thirdly, to find a spiritual experience. This last is so alluring and inviting that we have laid hold of it. And we know that this same spiritual experience awaits us ahead.
