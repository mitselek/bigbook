# Chapter 4: We Agnostics (p. 44)

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p001]
In the previous chapters you gained some idea of alcoholism. We hope we have made plain the difference between the alcoholic and the non-alcoholic. If you have decided you want what we have, and are willing to take the measures that lead to acquiring it, then you have already begun steps toward adopting certain attitudes. Along with them, you have the readiness to build a new life.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p002]
We told you that such codes and philosophies did not save us, however much we might try. We might wish to be moral, we might wish for philosophical comfort — indeed we might want all this with all our might — yet we had not the needed power. Our human resources, directed by our will, were not sufficient; they failed us utterly.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p003]
Our dilemma was lack of power. We had to find a power by which we could live, and it had to be a Power greater than ourselves. Plainly. But where and how were we to find this Power?

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p004]
Well, that's exactly what this book is about. The book's purpose is to help you find the Power greater than yourself that will solve your problem. To this end we have written a book whose content is both spiritual and moral. And that, of course, means we shall speak of God. Here the difficulties with agnostics begin. Often we sit and talk with a newcomer and see hope kindle in him as we discuss his alcoholic problems and describe our fellowship. But his face falls when we talk of spiritual matters, especially if we mention God, for we have now reopened a field of topics he thought he had nicely avoided or entirely ignored.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p005]
We know how he feels. We have shared his sincere doubt and prejudice. Some of us have been violently anti-religious. To others the word "God" summoned up a picture of Him that someone had tried to force upon him in childhood. Perhaps we discarded that picture at the time, finding it inadequate. In doing so we imagined we had rejected the idea of God altogether. We were troubled by the notion that belief in, and dependence upon, any Power greater than ourselves was somehow pathetic — even cowardly. We looked upon this world of people at war, the warring theological systems, and the inexplicable woe and misery, with deeply skeptical eyes. We looked askance at many who claimed to be linked with God. How could the Supreme Being have anything to do with all that? And who could possibly comprehend a Supreme Being anyway? Yet there were also moments when, entranced by the starry night, we caught ourselves in the thought: "Who created all this?" We felt awe and wonder, but the feeling was brief and soon gone.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p006]
Yes, we agnostic-natured people have had such thoughts and experiences. But we hasten to reassure you. We discovered that as soon as we were able to lay aside prejudice and express even only a willingness to believe in a Power greater than ourselves, we began to achieve first results, even though for all of us it was beyond our strength to define or understand that Power which is God.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p007]
To our great relief we found that we did not have to start from one another's conceptions of God. Our own, however imperfect, was enough to approach and make contact with Him. As soon as we admitted the possibility of a Creative Intelligence hidden in the totality of things — a Spirit of the Universe — a new feeling of strength and direction seized us, provided we also took other simple steps. We found that God does not impose too hard conditions on those who seek Him. To us the Realm of Spirit is broad, roomy, all-inclusive; never hemmed in nor forbidding to those who seek it sincerely. We believe it is open to all people.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p008]
Therefore when we talk to you of God, we mean by this your own conception of God. The same applies to other spiritual expressions you find in this book. Do not let any prejudices you may feel against spiritual terms keep you from honestly asking yourself what they mean for you. That was all we needed in the beginning to start growing spiritually — to make the first conscious contact with God as we understand Him. Later we found we were able to take in much that had at first seemed wholly out of reach. That was the growing, but if we wanted to grow, we had to start somewhere. So we used our own understanding, however limited.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p009]
We had only one short question to put to ourselves. "Do I now believe, or am I willing to believe, that there is a Power greater than myself?" As soon as a person can say he believes or is willing to believe, we lovingly affirm that he is on the right road. Among us it has been proved over and over that a wonderfully effective spiritual structure can be built on such a simple cornerstone.\*

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p010]
This was grand news for us, for we had thought that to use spiritual principles one must believe many hard-to-believe things. When people offered us spiritual approaches, we would often answer: "I want what this man has. I am sure it would work if only I believed as he believes. But I cannot accept as certain truths the religious convictions so evident to him." So it was a comfort to know we could start in a simpler way.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p011]
Besides our apparent lack of capacity to believe many things without proof, stubbornness, hypersensitivity, and unreasonable prejudice often stood in our way. Some of us were so touchy that even the casual mention of spiritual matters raised our hackles. That way of thinking had to go. Although some of us resisted, we did not find it too difficult to lay aside such feelings. Facing the havoc wrought by alcoholism, we soon became as open-minded on spiritual matters as we had tried to be on other questions. In this regard alcohol had a great power of persuasion. Its blows finally brought us to our senses. At times it was a wearisome process; we hope no one will remain in the grip of prejudice as long as some of us.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p012]
The reader may yet ask why he should believe in a Power greater than himself. In our view there are good reasons. Let us examine some of them.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p013]
The practical-minded man of today clings to facts and results. Nonetheless the twentieth century readily embraces all sorts of theories, provided they are founded on facts. We have, for example, several theories about electricity. Everyone believes them without the slightest shadow of doubt. What is the reason for so ready an acceptance? Simply that it is impossible to explain what we see, feel, direct, or use, without starting from some reasonable assumption.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p014]
Today people believe many hypotheses for which there is firm evidence, even though there is no fully visible proof. And does not science show that visible evidence is the weakest kind of proof? As a man investigates the material world, it is constantly shown that outward appearance does not correspond at all to inner reality. To illustrate:

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p015]
An ordinary steel beam consists of an enormous mass of electrons whirling around one another at incredible speed. These tiny bodies are governed by precise laws, and these laws hold for the entire material world. Science assures us of this. We have no reason to doubt it. But if one suggests the perfectly logical assumption that behind the material world and the life visible to our eyes there is yet an Almighty, Directing, Creative Intelligence, our perverse nature springs up at once and sets us busily persuading ourselves that this is not so. We read verbose books and enjoy high-flown arguments, thinking we believe that no God is needed to explain the universe. If our claims were true, it would follow that life arose from nothing, means nothing, and is heading nowhere.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p016]
Instead of regarding ourselves as intelligent agents, forerunners of God's ever-developing Creation, we agnostics and atheists preferred to believe that our human intelligence was the last word — the Alpha and Omega, the beginning and end of everything. Is this not vain thinking?

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p017]
We who have walked this road of doubt ask you to set aside your prejudices even regarding organized religion. We have become clear that, whatever human shortcomings those various religions may have, they have given millions of people aim and direction in life. Religious people have a logical conception of the nature of life. Truly we had no reasonable conception of our own before. We were in the habit of cynically dissecting spiritual beliefs and practices to amuse ourselves, while we might have noticed that many spiritual people of all races, colors, and creeds showed a steadfastness, happiness, and usefulness that we too ought to have striven for.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p018]
Instead we fastened our attention on their human shortcomings and used their failings at times to write them off entirely. We spoke of intolerance while being intolerant ourselves. We missed the forest's true reality and beauty because we allowed our attention to be diverted by the ugliness of a few trees. We never troubled to listen to the spiritual side of life.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p019]
From our personal stories you will see how different are the ways in which each narrator approaches and senses that Power greater than himself. Whether we agree with any particular approach or conception seems to matter little. Experience has taught us that for our purpose there is no need to worry about such questions. They are questions each person settles for himself.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p020]
On one point, however, these men and women were surprisingly united. Each has gained access to, and believes in, a Power greater than himself. This Power has wrought the humanly impossible in every person's life. As one famous American statesman said: "Let us look at the record."

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p021]
Here are thousands of truly ordinary men and women. They declare plainly that since they began to believe in a Power greater than themselves, and to relate to that Power in a certain way, and to do some simple things, a revolutionary change has taken place in their way of life and thought. Looking in the face of ruin and despair, standing face to face with the complete exhaustion of their human resources, they discovered that new strength, peace, a sense of happiness and purpose flowed into them. This happened soon after they had with all their heart fulfilled a few simple conditions. Once thoroughly crushed by the futility and worthlessness of their existence, these people are now wholly remade in their attitude to life and its problems.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p022]
We used to excuse our loss of control over alcohol by saying that problems were so heavy that no one could have kept a clear head to solve them. But now we see other people who face the same problems and even worse, yet remain sober. Alcohol no longer seems so important to them. Something more important has come into their lives in its place. Sobriety itself has become their priority.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p023]
Shouldn't that be reason enough to believe that the power which helps you may exist?

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p024]
\*Please read the further discussion of "Spiritual Experience" — Appendix II.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p025]
We who have had these experiences cannot believe that the world is going to get any better unless we ourselves change. First we must step aside from our old pattern of thinking; then problems recede of themselves. We have been shown how to step aside and let Him come to help. It is not essentially hard to give one's self wholly over to Him once conscious that nothing else has really helped us.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p026]
Of course we had difficulties besides agnosticism and atheism. Some of us had become cynics. Like scientists, who were often positive, they too thought everything was relative — one thing equal to another. It was experience that showed us that only a group as a whole began to help us, and we could no longer scoff at those who might help us anywhere. When we noticed a beginning, it was still in our man. Sometimes we thought we could flee and withdraw from such activity. But all this alacrity for our current way of life was seeming. Perhaps we never did fully control it — there is something about us that passes beyond generations, something in the saving interest of people themselves. Could this not be what God knows?

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p027]
We must not force ourselves to believe blindly. Although we were once alcoholics, we still exist in entirely earthly conditions. Many of us live lives of sound mind. God cannot be established as a definite, clear object; rather we must admit that it is largely our inner wisdom and level of perception. And certainly, all efforts put toward that end have enough result to make the labor worthwhile. One extreme repeats the other. We have stood convinced with our own eyes that if we devote ourselves to a spiritual understanding of God, our life changes completely. We stand at the peak of faith. Is this not as paradoxical as whether we remain incomplete or become bearers of the whole of faith? We had the honor to accept the word, and we are shrewd. Did our mind then forbid the changes that came from Him? When we sank to the point of asserting human power, does that same mind not declare to us that there is a Power higher than we are?

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p028]
As someone once said so beautifully: "We all have hope. We find enlightenment. We cannot say in whom it lies, but we believe it exists." And it lies within us all, and can join us to a higher purpose, if we open ourselves to the journey.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p029]
And to these spiritual experiences many wonders are added. There are changes in patterns of thought, in clarity, in priorities, and in our behavior.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p030]
We turned to Him. But how? And was it indeed the same God to whom we might have wished to turn? And how was it possible that we had so suddenly become attached to what we had hitherto kept away from? How do we keep on?

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p031]
As we became more spiritual, we found that we began to follow a natural path. Things were simpler. All began to work by itself. Our problems receded or became acceptable to us as, at the same time, we found contentment with our own selves. Unpleasant things were no longer our new friends. Thus softly does a transparent change of life come about. Once He goes to work, there is no turning back to the old way.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p032]
This was only the beginning of the unusual things to be told. Our whole way of thinking changed. Those things we used as excuses to drink were the substance of our life. If life itself is not our best, was it not better to drink? Should we have lived somehow hand-to-mouth, frugally? Certainly not! Look at all the former boozers who now are trying to condition the whole of life as subconscious. And it was much more than we had hoped or imagined. We wonder whether all this might have had reasons that came our way. In a sense we came far down the road. We looked back and thought that we would have set ourselves on such a road, or such a program of development, perhaps much sooner.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p033]
To sacrifice something petty for the sake of something more useful, even though one would never wish to do so. Solving the problem, we have solved our dreams and soaring upward glimpses. All the people of the world find their experiences judged, and this is no less true for recovery from alcoholism.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p034]
By a chance turn, have you not sometimes wished to have been born different? Would you not have wished to be more important, to get on more easily with people, a better companion to all? Have you not sensed that the world around you is still waiting for you? Do you know that it is possible to stand at your full height before God, and all that we have until now left owing is now in His hands? God may yet not be the witness to that truth by which we generally judge ourselves in moments of memory. As something new, we learn it past the everyday experiences. If we take the same stance as our countryman Bill took, shall we forget, then, what was our former imbalance?

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p035]
Remembering old times, we knew we could not go on in this way. We had to make an effort. "Grip tightly and keep the pressure on," our friend said. "Sometimes ride lightly. Sometimes be a truster of God. Do not take yourself too seriously. Carry your thoughts and attitudes in balance. Thus you will win." This we did, and more. And it worked.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p036]
It works — if we are truly willing to try it. It works fully. So much we believe.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p037]
Besides, Alcoholics Anonymous also works for him. He has recovered.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p038]
It is great news that once-hopeless alcoholics are able to return to normal life. Scarcely any of us would have imagined that without a change of worldview and an inward reversal of thinking habits, recovery would be possible.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p039]
Alcoholics are indeed apt to think that they are written off. They and their families do suffer. Someone may tell them it is hopeless, and they are set to flee and find refuge here.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p040]
But we believe we would not be doing justice if we focused on clearly outlined cases or claims. We have simply wanted to note that the alcoholic has trouble, and that as such it can be overcome. We help all who take an interest in recovery. He may differ socially and economically from others, but now it is no longer a disease, since he has either cured the disease once or received a vaccine against it.

::para[ch04-meie-agnostikud-p041]
So it is up to you. Do you still trouble yourself with agnostic conceptions? Did you take up all the spiritual as though it were an interview? If you still say that was so, then come and join us on the road that has light.
